01 — The Problem

A portal patients avoided using

HealthTrack's patient portal had been built piecemeal over six years, with features bolted on by different teams without a unified design vision. The result was an app that patients found confusing, slow, and untrustworthy — especially when it came to managing appointments and reviewing test results.

The business was seeing a 44% appointment booking abandonment rate through the app, with support tickets flooding in about "can't find my results" and "the app logged me out mid-booking." The App Store rating had fallen to 2.9★.

02 — Research & Discovery

Listening before designing

I began with a two-week discovery sprint. I conducted 18 contextual interviews with patients across age groups, reviewed 6 months of App Store reviews and support tickets, and ran a heuristic evaluation of the existing app against Nielsen's usability heuristics.

I also shadowed two patient coordinators at a clinic to observe how patients used — or avoided using — the app during real appointments. This gave me crucial context that interviews alone wouldn't surface.

03 — Define

Synthesising what we learned

After research synthesis, three core user needs emerged clearly. Patients needed to quickly understand what actions they needed to take. They needed to trust the system with their health data. And they needed to complete tasks in one go — without confusion or dead ends forcing them to call the clinic instead.

I defined two primary personas: "Active Anya," a 34-year-old managing a chronic condition who needed frequent app use, and "Occasional Oliver," a 61-year-old who only used the app for annual checkups and needed the experience to be self-explanatory every time.

👩‍💼

Active Anya, 34

Frequent User

Manages Type 2 diabetes. Uses the app weekly to log vitals, review lab results, and message her care team. Frustrated by inconsistent navigation and a home screen that never changes despite her frequent use.

Goal: Get to her data fast, without re-learning the app each visit.

👨

Occasional Oliver, 61

Infrequent User

Comes in once a year for a physical. Needs to book appointments, review results, and pay his copay. Forgets how the app works between visits and finds the information density overwhelming.

Goal: Complete a specific task quickly without getting lost or anxious.

How might we help patients feel in control of their health journey — so the app becomes a trusted companion rather than a confusing barrier?

04 — Ideate

From concepts to direction

I ran two design studio sessions with the PM and engineers to rapidly sketch solutions. I generated over 60 concept sketches across three how-might-we questions, then dot-voted with the team to identify the most promising directions.

Three key design directions emerged: a "Today" home screen that surfaces only what's urgent and actionable; a simplified appointment booking flow condensed from 8 steps to 3; and a results view designed to reduce anxiety by leading with plain-language summaries before technical values.

05 — Prototype & Test

Validating before building

I built a mid-fidelity interactive prototype in Figma covering the three primary flows: onboarding, appointment booking, and results viewing. I ran two rounds of moderated usability tests — six participants each — using UserZoom for remote sessions and Maze for unmoderated click-path analysis.

Round one surfaced a critical issue: the "Today" home screen was missing a clear way to access past appointments, leading 4 of 6 participants to get lost when trying to find previous visit notes. I iterated quickly and the second round showed a 94% task completion rate across all three flows.

06 — Final Design

A portal patients want to open

The final design established a clear visual hierarchy anchored around the patient's immediate needs. The home screen surfaces upcoming appointments, unread results, and any pending actions — nothing more. Navigation is persistent and clear. The booking flow is three steps, and the results view leads with a plain-English summary before presenting any numbers.

I also built a comprehensive component library in Figma to support the engineering team and ensure consistency across future feature additions — covering 84 components across 12 categories, all with built-in accessibility tokens.

08 — Reflection

What I'd do differently

If I were starting over, I'd involve the engineering team in the research synthesis phase earlier. There were a few design decisions that required significant re-work once the engineers pointed out underlying system constraints — constraints that, had I known about them earlier, would have led me toward equally good but simpler solutions.

I'd also invest more time in the onboarding experience for infrequent users like Oliver. The post-launch data showed that users who only opened the app a few times a year still struggled during those visits — an area we've since prioritised in the roadmap.
