01 — Context & Problem

A powerful tool no one wanted to open

Nexus was a data analytics platform used by 600+ data analysts across a large retail enterprise. The underlying data and computation engine were genuinely powerful. But years of feature additions by multiple teams had left the UI in disarray — a tangled web of nested menus, inconsistent components, and dashboards that loaded slowly and disclosed information poorly.

Analysts were working around the tool: exporting raw data into Excel, building shadow dashboards in Tableau, or simply asking colleagues to run queries on their behalf. Productivity was suffering, and the platform team was fielding a growing backlog of complaints.

02 — Research

Understanding expert mental models

Enterprise UX research is different. Users are domain experts who've built deep (often dysfunctional) habits around existing tools. The risk is designing something "better" in theory but that breaks established workflows. I spent the first six weeks doing deep contextual research before touching a single wireframe.

I ran 26 contextual inquiry sessions — sitting beside analysts while they worked — and supplemented this with FullStory session recordings, a IA card sorting exercise with 40 participants, and a tree testing exercise to validate navigation hypotheses before committing to them.

03 — Information Architecture

From 340 nav items to 24

The IA redesign was the highest-leverage single decision of the project. Based on card sort analysis, I reorganised the navigation around five task-oriented categories that matched how analysts actually thought about their work: Explore (ad hoc querying), Monitor (dashboards and alerts), Build (creating new reports), Share (collaboration), and Settings.

This reduced the global nav from 340 items to 24, with contextual sub-navigation appearing inline within each section. Tree testing showed an 88% correct navigation rate on the new structure, up from 31% on the original.

04 — Design System

Building the component library

Before designing screens, I audited the existing UI and identified 140+ unique button variants, 60+ different table styles, and an inconsistent colour system with no documented tokens. The fragmentation was a symptom of the same root cause as the IA problem: no shared design language.

I built Nexus Design System (NDS) — a Figma library of 200 components across 15 categories, paired with a token system using semantic naming (e.g., --color-action-primary rather than #8B5CF6). This enabled the engineers to implement theming and dark mode later without design changes.

05 — Final Design

A dashboard analysts want to open

The redesigned Nexus puts the analyst's current task front and center. A persistent command palette (Cmd+K) lets power users navigate without touching the mouse. Charts use a consistent visual grammar with clear empty states, loading states, and error states — the small things that the original had never gotten right.

Most importantly, the most common workflow — loading a saved query, adjusting filters, and exporting results — was reduced from 18 clicks to 5, with keyboard shortcut support throughout.

06 — Developer Handoff

Bridging design and engineering

With 6 engineers on the team, clear handoff was non-negotiable. I moved beyond "export specs from Figma" and instead ran three structured handoff sessions, walking through each section of the product with the full engineering team before they started building.

I documented every interaction state — hover, active, focus, loading, empty, error — in a dedicated Figma file linked to the component library. I also built a lightweight decision log capturing why certain design choices were made, so engineers could make informed trade-offs during implementation without coming back to me for every edge case.

08 — Reflection

What makes enterprise UX hard

Enterprise design taught me that the hardest problems are often organisational, not visual. The Nexus UI was bad because teams had been adding features without a design system or shared governance for years. The design system I built wasn't just a deliverable — it was an attempt to change how the product team operated.

The most unexpected challenge was change management with users. Analysts who had built muscle memory around the old (broken) navigation were genuinely frustrated during the transition period, even though the new design objectively outperformed. I learned to build "transition support" — contextual tooltips and a help overlay — into every major navigation change I make now.
