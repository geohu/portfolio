01 — The Opportunity

Travel planning is broken for groups

Planning a trip solo is hard enough. Planning with a group — reconciling different preferences, budgets, and schedules — is a logistical nightmare that typically plays out across WhatsApp threads, Google Docs, spreadsheets, and frantic emails, with someone inevitably taking on a disproportionate planning burden.

The founding team at Waypoint had identified this pain point, but needed a designer to shape what the solution actually looks and feels like. I joined as the sole designer at the concept stage, with a rough idea and a tight brief: build something people would use repeatedly and recommend to friends.

02 — Research

Understanding real planning behaviour

Before sketching a single screen, I led three weeks of generative research. I conducted 24 in-depth interviews with frequent travellers, recruited a mix of solo travellers, couples, and friend groups of 3–8 people. I also ran a competitive teardown of 8 apps in the travel and planning space — analysing where they succeeded and where they fell short.

The most valuable research came from asking participants to share their most recent group trip planning process with me, step by step. The patterns that emerged were consistent and striking.

"I made a shared notes doc at first, but people just… didn't open it. So I ended up just planning it myself. Every time."

— Interview participant, frequent group traveller

03 — Define

The product opportunity space

Based on research synthesis, I mapped three distinct user archetypes: The Organiser (takes the lead, needs tools to delegate and track), The Contributor (wants to participate but hates being overwhelmed), and The Along-for-the-Ride (just needs to show up with the right info at the right time).

Designing for all three meant creating clear roles and permissions, a low-friction contribution model for passive members, and a beautifully simple "day of" view that reduced the app to just what you need right now.

How might we make group trip planning feel effortless — so every member feels involved without placing the full burden on one person?

— Product Design Principle

04 — Design

Core interactions & visual language

The visual language was designed to feel like travel itself: open, inviting, and full of possibility. I used a card-based layout so that trip plans felt tangible — like a physical itinerary you could pick up and rearrange. Full-bleed destination imagery makes the experience emotionally resonant, not just functional.

The three core surfaces were the Trip Board (a shareable overview all members can access), the Itinerary (a day-by-day schedule with collaborative editing), and the Discovery Layer (a map and suggestion engine to help groups find and vote on places).

05 — Prototype & Testing

Testing with real groups

A key methodological choice was to test Waypoint with actual groups, not isolated individuals. I recruited five friend groups of 3–5 people each and asked them to plan a real upcoming trip using Waypoint over a two-week period, then interviewed each group afterward.

This surfaced issues that single-user testing would never catch — like the fact that the voting feature confused users who received the invite notification but hadn't downloaded the app yet, and that the Organiser role needed clearer status indicators so they knew what was still pending action.

06 — Outcomes

Six months post-launch

Waypoint launched on the App Store in December 2024. Growth was driven primarily by word of mouth — the viral loop of inviting group members into a shared trip proved powerful, with 62% of new users joining via a shared trip link rather than organic discovery.

07 — Reflection

Building 0→1 is humbling

The biggest lesson from Waypoint was how much product scope matters. In the early months, every stakeholder meeting produced another "what if we also…" feature. Staying disciplined about what the core use case was — and what it wasn't — was the hardest part of the role, and required me to develop skills in stakeholder communication I hadn't needed as much in redesign projects.

I'm also proud of the group testing methodology. It felt risky to recruit real groups and give them a genuinely unfinished product, but the quality of insights it produced made every subsequent design decision sharper and better grounded in actual behaviour.
